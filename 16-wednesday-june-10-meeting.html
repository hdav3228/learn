What I Learned

The full path through the meeting

The important shift was from finding one best parameter value to describing uncertainty over every plausible value. The formulas below are a compact reference for that path.

1. ModelState how the observed data could have been generated.
2. LikelihoodScore parameter values by how well they explain the observations.
3. PriorRepresent what was plausible before seeing these observations.
4. PosteriorCombine the likelihood and prior, then normalize.
5. ComputeDerive the posterior exactly when possible; otherwise approximate it with MCMC.
6. InterpretUse the distribution, not only its mean, to discuss evidence and uncertainty.
QuestionPoint estimate answerBayesian answer
What is the parameter?One best value, often the MLE.A posterior distribution over plausible values.
How uncertain am I?Requires a separate sampling-distribution argument.Read uncertainty from the posterior, subject to the model and prior.
Can I solve it directly?Sometimes optimization is straightforward.Sometimes conjugacy gives a formula; otherwise use numerical integration or MCMC.

Bayes Rule

p(θ | y) = p(y | θ) π(θ)∫ p(y | θ) π(θ) dθ = likelihood × priormarginal likelihood
TermMeaningQuestion it answers
p(y | θ)LikelihoodIf the parameter were θ, how compatible would the observed data be?
π(θ)PriorWhat parameter values were plausible before these data?
p(θ | y)PosteriorAfter seeing the data, what values remain plausible and with what probability density?
p(y)Marginal likelihood/evidenceWhat constant makes the posterior integrate to one? It also supports model comparison.
The phrase to remember
The posterior is proportional to likelihood times prior. The denominator does not depend on θ, but it matters because it turns the proportional expression into a proper probability distribution.

Normal Likelihood And MLE

Assume independent observations x_1, ..., x_n come from a Normal distribution:

xi | μ, σ2 ∼ Normal(μ, σ2)

Likelihood

L(μ, σ2) = ∏i=1n 1√(2πσ2) exp[-(xi-μ)2/(2σ2)]

Every observation contributes one density value, so independence turns the joint likelihood into a product.

Log-likelihood

ℓ = -n2log(2π) - n log(σ) - 12σ2 ∑(xi-μ)2

The log changes products into sums and makes differentiation and numerical computation easier.

μ̂MLE = x̄ = 1n∑xi      σ̂2MLE = 1n∑(xi-x̄)2
Easy derivation mistake from the meeting
Terms that are constant with respect to μ can be dropped while differentiating by μ, but the -n log(σ) term cannot be dropped when differentiating by σ. What counts as a constant depends on the parameter being optimized.

Normal Posterior With Known Variance

The clean first Bayesian extension treats σ2 as known and places a Normal prior on the unknown mean.

xi | μ ∼ Normal(μ, σ2)     μ ∼ Normal(μ0, τ02)
μ | x ∼ Normal(μn, τn2)

Posterior variance

τn2 = (1τ02 + nσ2)-1

Precisions add. More data increases precision and narrows the posterior.

Posterior mean

μn = τn2 (μ0τ02 + n x̄σ2)

The posterior mean is a precision-weighted compromise between the prior mean and sample mean.

Why conjugacy is useful
A Normal prior combined with this Normal likelihood gives a Normal posterior. The distribution family stays the same; only its parameters are updated.

Unknown Mean And Variance

When both the mean and variance are unknown, the prior has to describe them jointly. One common conjugate choice uses the precision λ = 1/σ2.

μ | λ ∼ Normal(μ0, (κ0λ)-1)     λ ∼ Gamma(α0, β0)
Updated parameterFormulaWhat changed
κnκ0 + nPrior effective sample size plus observed sample size.
μn(κ0μ0 + n x̄) / κnWeighted prior and observed means.
αnα0 + n/2Gamma shape updated by the amount of data.
βn β0 + 0.5∑(xi-x̄)2 + [κ0n(x̄-μ0)2]/[2κn] Combines within-sample variation and disagreement between the prior mean and sample mean.
Parameterization warning
Gamma distributions can be written with a rate or a scale parameter, so formulas can look different across papers and notes. Before using an update equation, check exactly how Gamma(α, β) is defined. A Normal-inverse-chi-squared prior is an analogous alternative written in terms of variance instead of precision.

Beta-Binomial Coin Example

Liu and Lawrence use coin flips because the full posterior can be derived exactly. Let θ be the probability of heads, with h observed heads and t observed tails.

Likelihood

p(y | θ) = θh(1-θ)t

The order is ignored in this version; only the numbers of heads and tails matter.

Beta prior

θ ∼ Beta(α, β) ∝ θα-1(1-θ)β-1

α and β act like prior pseudo-counts.

Posterior

θ | y ∼ Beta(α+h, β+t)

The Beta prior is conjugate to the Bernoulli/binomial likelihood.

Posterior mean

E[θ | y] = α+hα+β+h+t

The answer combines prior counts and observed counts.

The assigned example: 12 heads and 18 tails

1
MLE: 12/30 = 0.4. This is one best point estimate.
2
Uniform prior: choose Beta(1,1), which gives equal prior density across 0 to 1.
3
Posterior: Beta(13,19).
4
Posterior mean: 13/32 = 0.40625. More important than this one number is the distribution's spread.
5
Posterior uncertainty: the central 95% credible interval is approximately [0.245, 0.578], and P(θ ≥ 0.5 | y) ≈ 0.141.
P(θ ≥ 0.5 | y) = ∫0.51 Beta(θ; 13, 19) dθ
Why this example matters for GenomegaMap
The coin problem has a closed-form posterior, while GenomegaMap's joint posterior is too complicated to normalize analytically. The goal is still the same: represent plausible parameter values and their uncertainty. MCMC supplies the approximation when conjugate algebra does not.

MCMC And GenomegaMap

GenomegaMap uses Metropolis-Hastings updates to explore its posterior. The acceptance ratio balances the proposed and current posterior densities with the forward and reverse proposal probabilities. This can accept some lower-density moves, preventing the chain from becoming a simple optimizer.

a = min(1, p(θ' | y) q(θ | θ') p(θ | y) q(θ' | θ) )
Trace featureWhat it meansWhat I should do
Repeated valuesA proposed update was rejected, so the chain kept its previous state.Do not treat repeated rows as a file error by themselves.
Stable wanderingThe chain is exploring a consistent high-posterior region.Inspect trace plots and compare independent chains when available.
Trend or long driftThe chain may not have reached its stationary distribution.Question convergence before interpreting intervals.
Wide posteriorThe model/data leave substantial parameter uncertainty.Do not automatically call this failed convergence; diagnose the trace separately.
Narrower posterior with more dataThe likelihood contributes more information.Expect stronger concentration, while remembering model assumptions still matter.

The GenomegaMap likelihood bridge

GenomegaMap models allele counts at each codon using a Dirichlet-multinomial approximation. Conditional on a possible oldest allele A, the per-site likelihood has this form:

Pr(x | A) = n!∏jxj! B(x + aA)B(aA)     Pr(x) = ∑A pA Pr(x | A)

Here x is the vector of observed allele counts at a codon and B is the multivariate beta function. The method combines site likelihoods under an independence approximation and samples substitution parameters by MCMC. This is why the output log-likelihood is a large negative number: software sums log probabilities across many sites instead of multiplying many tiny probabilities directly.

What the posterior cannot prove
A posterior is conditional on the model, prior, data quality, and cohort design. A clean MCMC trace does not remove lineage, resistance, geography, or sampling confounding, and a high omega0 does not by itself prove adaptation to diabetes.

How To Read The Papers

A research skill emphasized in the meeting

The goal is not to read every paper linearly or memorize every equation. I need to build a knowledge structure that lets me recognize which sentence, assumption, or formula answers the question in front of me.

PassWhat I look forQuestion I should be able to answer
1. PurposeAbstract, introduction, final discussion.What problem does this paper solve, and why does it matter to my project?
2. ModelObserved data, unknown parameters, likelihood, and priors.What is random, what is assumed, and what is inferred?
3. ComputationClosed-form derivation, dynamic programming, Gibbs, or Metropolis-Hastings.How does the paper turn the model into an answer?
4. EvidenceFigures, tables, diagnostics, comparisons, and validation.What result supports the main claim, and what would weaken it?
5. Project linkOne formula, one assumption, and one limitation worth carrying forward.How does this change what I run, inspect, or say about my data?
My formula-reading checklist
For each important equation, identify the left-hand-side target, every symbol on the right, which terms come from the data, which terms come from assumptions or priors, and whether the expression is normalized, proportional, or only an approximation.

What I Can Say

One-minute version

The June 10 lesson connected maximum likelihood to Bayesian inference. With a Normal example, I can derive the MLEs for the mean and variance, then show how a prior changes the result from one best value into a posterior distribution. Conjugate examples such as Normal-Normal and Beta-binomial have direct update formulas. For the paper's coin example, 12 heads and 18 tails with a uniform prior gives a Beta(13,19) posterior, so I can quantify uncertainty around the 0.4 observed proportion. GenomegaMap has the same inferential goal, but its Dirichlet-multinomial codon model and joint parameter space are too complicated for a closed form, so it uses Metropolis-Hastings MCMC. The traces are approximate posterior samples, and I need to interpret their spread separately from convergence diagnostics and biological confounding.
