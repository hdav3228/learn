Paper 7 · Mejia-Ponce et al. 2023 · Mexico WGS context

Genomic epidemiology of drug-resistant M. tuberculosis in Mexico

This paper provides the whole-genome sequencing background for the Mexican isolates in our project. It explains the lineage structure (dominated by L4 Haarlem/X-type), the high rate of drug resistance, and the problem of genotype-phenotype discordance.

Methods Lineages Discordance
Read this first — in plain English If you pull 85 clinical TB genomes from Mexico, what do they look like? This paper shows they are almost entirely "Lineage 4" (the Euro-American lineage), heavily skewed toward Haarlem and X-type sublineages, and frequently drug-resistant. It also notes that type 2 diabetes was the most common comorbidity in the sampled patients. For our GenomegaMap project, this paper is a giant warning sign: if we find a "diabetes-specific" mutation, we must be absolutely certain it isn't just a Haarlem-specific lineage marker or an uncharacterized drug-resistance mutation.

2. Methods decoder: WGS and Profiling

How they analyzed the genomes

The paper combines phenotypic lab testing with whole-genome sequencing to classify lineages, build phylogenetic trees, and predict drug resistance.

Method stepWhat it doesWhy it matters for us
Phenotypic DST Lab tests exposing bacteria to first-line drugs (H, R, E, S, Z) to see if they grow. This is the "ground truth" phenotype that the genomic predictions are judged against.
WGS Pipeline Illumina sequencing achieving high mean coverage (~93.8X). High coverage ensures the variant calls are trustworthy, which GenomegaMap relies on.
TBProfiler A software tool that scans a TB genome and assigns a lineage and predicts drug resistance based on known mutations. We use TBProfiler outputs in our own pipeline to verify lineage and exclude mixed isolates.
Phylogeny Building an evolutionary tree from SNPs, excluding repetitive regions (PE/PPE) and known resistance genes. Excluding resistance genes prevents the tree from artificially clustering unrelated resistant strains together.

3. Results & Lineages

The dominant lineage was Euro-American L4, representing about 94% of the dataset. Two common sublineages were especially prominent: Haarlem 4.1.2.1 and X-type 4.1.1.3.

L4 Dominance

80 of 85 samples were Lineage 4. This reflects historical colonization and global TB distribution patterns.

Haarlem (4.1.2.1)

A major sublineage (n=32). Highly prevalent in this Mexican cohort.

X-type (4.1.1.3)

Another major sublineage (n=34). The paper notes it as endemic to Mexico.

Comorbidity

Type 2 diabetes was the most common comorbidity, reported for 29 of the patients.

Worked Interpretation: Lineage Confounding

1
Scenario: GenomegaMap flags an unknown gene as highly selected in our diabetes cohort compared to the non-diabetes cohort.
2
The lineage check: You look at the metadata for the two cohorts. You realize the diabetes cohort is 90% Haarlem lineage, while the non-diabetes cohort is 90% X-type lineage.
3
The risk: The gene might not have anything to do with diabetes. It might just be a standard genetic difference that separates the Haarlem evolutionary branch from the X-type branch.
4
The solution: This is why the paper's lineage breakdown matters. If we don't balance lineages across our clinical groups (using TBProfiler sublineage outputs), we will report false positives driven by evolutionary history, not host environment.

4. Phenotype/genotype discordance

When the lab and the computer disagree

Phenotypic testing found 34 resistant strains, but the canonical genotype predictions didn't always match the lab results.

Phenotype (Lab)

The bacteria grew in the presence of the drug. We know it's resistant, but we don't necessarily know *why*.

Genotype (Computer)

Software like TBProfiler looks for known mutations (e.g., in *katG* or *rpoB*). If it finds them, it predicts resistance.

The paper notes discrepancies—for example, pyrazinamide-monoresistant strains that lacked known resistance mutations in the pncA gene. This means there are unknown resistance mechanisms at play in this population.

Worked Interpretation: The Uncharacterized Resistance Trap

1
Scenario: GenomegaMap flags a hypothetical gene as positively selected in our diabetes cohort.
2
The discordance context: Ballell taught us that cell-wall targets are key, and this paper shows that uncharacterized drug resistance exists in this specific Mexican population.
3
The risk: If diabetic patients in this cohort received different drug regimens (or had worse adherence leading to resistance), our "diabetes" selection hit might actually be a novel, uncharacterized drug resistance mutation.
4
The check: We must cross-reference the phenotypic DST data in the metadata to ensure the diabetes cohort isn't heavily skewed with resistant isolates.

5. Talk Track

What to say in a meeting

"Our Mexican cohort, originally detailed by Mejia-Ponce, is heavily skewed toward the L4 Euro-American lineage, specifically the Haarlem and X-type sublineages. Furthermore, almost 40% of the isolates showed phenotypic drug resistance. Therefore, any diabetes-specific selection hits we find must be rigorously cross-checked to ensure we aren't just accidentally detecting an L4-specific SNP or an uncharacterized drug resistance mutation masquerading as a metabolic adaptation."

6. What not to overclaim

What NOT to overclaim
  • Haarlem != Diabetes: Do not use this paper to claim that Haarlem is *the* diabetes-causing strain. Lineage prevalence is driven by geography and history, not just host comorbidity.
  • Isolate lists: Do not use the raw isolate list from this paper directly—always use our filtered `samplestatus=good` workbook to avoid poor-quality genomes.
