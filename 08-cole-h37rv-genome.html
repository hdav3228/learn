1. Why this paper matters

Project Relevance

When GenomegaMap outputs a hit like "Rv1448c", this is the paper that explains what the "Rv" stands for and how we know what that gene does. It sets the baseline coordinate system for all variant calling and gene-level dN/dS estimation.

Genome size 4.41 Mb H37Rv complete sequence: 4,411,529 bp.
Genes ~4,000 Approximate protein-coding gene count.
Signature biology Lipids Large coding capacity devoted to lipogenesis and lipolysis.

The coordinate system

All later Rv gene IDs, selection hits, and variant calls sit on this H37Rv annotation backbone. Without it, "Rv1448c" is just a string.

The biological framework

Cole classified genes into metabolism, regulation, virulence, cell-wall, PE/PPE, and unknown categories. When GenomegaMap flags a gene, Cole tells you the first-pass functional category.

3. Methods decoder

Why this section matters

Understanding how the genome was sequenced and annotated tells you what the annotations can and cannot promise. Cole 1998 used cosmid library shotgun sequencing — state-of-the-art for 1998 but primitive by modern standards.

Method stepWhat they didWhy it matters now
Sequencing Shotgun sequencing of ordered cosmid and BAC clones covering the entire H37Rv chromosome. This gave a single complete reference. Modern WGS reads are mapped back to this reference.
Gene prediction Used computational ORF finding combined with codon-usage statistics, favored by the high G+C content (65.6%). High G+C makes start-codon prediction harder. Some gene boundaries may be slightly off in the original annotation.
Functional annotation Assigned functions by BLAST similarity to known proteins; many genes were "hypothetical" or "conserved hypothetical." ~40% of genes lacked clear function. Many have since been characterized, but always verify against modern databases like Mycobrowser.
G+C content 65.6% overall, with local deviations marking inserted elements or horizontally transferred regions. High G+C shapes codon usage bias, which directly affects the πj parameter in GenomegaMap's codon model.

4. Genome architecture

The paper reports a complete H37Rv genome of 4,411,529 bp, high G+C content (65.6%), and roughly 4,000 genes. It frames TB as a slow-growing, genetically homogeneous pathogen with a complex cell envelope and unusual intracellular biology.

Reference coordinate system

Later Rv gene IDs and selection hits sit on this H37Rv annotation backbone.

Functional categories

The paper classifies genes into metabolism, regulation, virulence, cell-wall, PE/PPE, and unknown categories.

tRNAs and codon bias

The genome includes tRNAs recognizing many sense codons, and high G+C content shapes codon use — relevant to GenomegaMap's πj parameter.

Unknown genes

Many genes were poorly characterized, which still affects modern selection-hit interpretation. "Hypothetical protein" is not your failure — it's the field's.

5. Lipid and cell-wall biology

Why TB is weird

Cole 1998 emphasizes that TB devotes an unusually large part of its coding capacity (almost 9%) to lipid metabolism, including enzymes for lipogenesis, lipolysis, mycolic acids, and related cell-wall structures. No other bacterium comes close.

This matters for the diabetes project because selected genes are not just abstract IDs. Many TB adaptation stories eventually run through metabolism, cell-wall remodeling, drug entry, immune interaction, or stress survival. Understanding the lipid emphasis helps you build biologically plausible stories for GenomegaMap hits.

Worked Interpretation: Finding a lipid-adjacent hit

1
Scenario: GenomegaMap flags an uncharacterized gene like Rv1490 as under positive selection in diabetics.
2
Action: Cross-reference it with the Cole 1998 functional categories or modern Mycobrowser. Check if it's adjacent to lipid metabolism genes on the genome map.
3
Interpretation: If it's in or near lipid metabolism, you can hypothesize that the diabetic host environment (which has altered fatty acid and glucose levels) is forcing TB to remodel its cell wall for better survival. This is a biologically plausible story, not a proven mechanism.
4
Caution: "Adjacent to lipid genes on the genome" is circumstantial. TB operons don't always cluster by function. Always check Mycobrowser or STRING for functional evidence.

6. PE/PPE/PGRS families

Why this section matters

PE/PPE/PGRS genes make up ~10% of the genome but are the single biggest source of false positives in selection scans. Understanding why they're problematic saves you from reporting noise as signal.

Cole 1998 highlights two glycine-rich repetitive protein families: PE and PPE, making up about 10% of the genome's coding capacity. Many PE proteins include PGRS (polymorphic GC-rich repetitive sequence) regions.

FamilyWhat to remember
PENamed for Pro-Glu motifs near the N terminus; includes a large PE-PGRS subgroup.
PPENamed for Pro-Pro-Glu motifs; another expanded repetitive family.
PGRSPolymorphic GC-rich repetitive sequences; variation can complicate mapping and interpretation.
PE/PPE genes and GenomegaMap: a practical warning
In the genome maps provided by the paper, there are large clusters of PE/PPE genes. These are highly variable and repetitive. For our GenomegaMap scans, these genes often show an artificially high ω because short reads misalign across the repeats, creating fake SNPs. We typically filter them out. If you see a PE/PPE gene at the top of your selection scan, be deeply skeptical before reporting it.

Worked Interpretation: A PE_PGRS gene appears in your top hits

1
Scenario: After running GenomegaMap on the diabetes cohort, a gene named PE_PGRS33 (Rv1818c) comes back with ω = 5.2.
2
Red flag: PE_PGRS genes are in Cole's repetitive family. The high ω is almost certainly a mapping artifact from short reads misaligning across GC-rich repeats.
3
Action: Exclude it from your candidate list. Note in your report that PE/PPE/PGRS genes were excluded per standard practice because of known short-read alignment artifacts.
4
What to say: "PE/PPE genes are a known source of false positives in dN/dS scans due to read-mapping artifacts in repetitive regions. We followed standard practice and excluded them from our candidate list."

8. Talk track

What to say in a meeting

"All of our variant calls are mapped to the H37Rv genome published by Cole in 1998. When we see a hit in GenomegaMap, we categorize its function based on the framework Cole laid out — specifically looking for enrichments in lipid metabolism, which makes up almost 10% of the TB genome, or filtering out the highly repetitive PE/PPE families that Cole identified. We always apply the Nature erratum corrections when discussing specific gene labels."

9. What not to overclaim

What NOT to overclaim
  • Functional annotations: Do not treat the original 1998 functional annotations as absolute ground truth. The sequence (the coordinate system) is the bedrock, but the functional annotations have been vastly updated. Always verify uncharacterized "hypothetical" genes against modern databases like Mycobrowser.
  • Gene count: The "~4,000" number is approximate. Modern re-annotations have refined the exact count, but the order of magnitude hasn't changed.
  • PE/PPE function: Cole noted these families but their functions are still debated. Do not claim they are "immune evasion genes" without citing more recent evidence.
