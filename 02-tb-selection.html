TB Selection Scans: applying GenomegaMap to clinical M. tuberculosis

Tuberculosis kills ~1.25 M people a year and infects a quarter of the planet. Two large clinical isolate collections — Moldova and the CRyPTIC consortium — give us tens of thousands of fully sequenced TB genomes. Run GenomegaMap across them at three different spatial resolutions and you can read off, gene by gene and codon by codon, where the bug is being pushed by drug pressure, host immunity, and metabolic stress.

In plain English The foundational paper (GenomegaMap) gave you a microscope. This Ioerger lab paper is what happens when you point that microscope at thousands of real TB genomes from sick patients around the world. The output is a ranked list of "which proteins is TB actively reshaping" — most obviously, the targets of antibiotics (drug‑resistance hotspots), but also surprising hits in metabolism and stress response. Each hit is a candidate drug target.

1. Why M. tuberculosis is the perfect test case

Why this section matters

Selection analysis works much better on some organisms than others. Before we apply GenomegaMap to TB, you need to understand the four reasons TB is the ideal organism for this method — they're also the four assumptions you'll later have to check before applying selection scans elsewhere (HIV, malaria, etc.).

M.tb has a genetic personality that makes selection analysis unusually clean — and unusually important.

Clonal, not recombining

Almost no horizontal gene transfer. SNPs accumulate vertically — every isolate is an independent draw from the same evolutionary process.

Slow mutation rate

~0.5 SNPs/genome/year. Most pairwise differences between isolates are tiny (often < 100 SNPs). Phylogenies are nearly star‑shaped.

Compact genome

~4,400 genes, ~4.4 Mb, only 10% non‑coding. Almost everything you look at is a protein‑coding region.

Massive sequencing

Tens of thousands of WGS isolates exist worldwide. Power to detect ω > 1 even at single‑codon resolution.

Crucially, M.tb has known biology — we already know rpoB / katG / gyrA / pncA / embB carry drug‑resistance mutations. So when you sweep an unbiased selection scan over the genome and those genes light up, that's not a discovery — it's a positive control. It's how Ioerger & Shatby validate the method, then look at what else lights up.

Mental model
Think of M.tb as a laboratory of natural selection running in human populations. Patients are the test tubes; antibiotics are the selection pressures; whole‑genome sequencing is the readout. GenomegaMap converts that readout into a Manhattan plot of evolutionary signal.

2. The two big datasets

Why this section matters

Every selection analysis you'll see in TB papers is run on one of two collections — or both. This section explains what those collections are, why they matter, and how dataset choice affects generalizability.

The foundational paper explained how GenomegaMap works. Now we apply it. Two clinical isolate collections dominate the M.tb selection literature:

Moldova (NCBI BioProject)
2057 isolates

A near‑exhaustive sample of TB cases from a single country with a high MDR/XDR burden. Genetically diverse — Lineage 2 (Beijing) is dominant, Lineage 4 also present. Drug‑resistance phenotypes documented for most isolates.

  • Genes flagged with ω > 1: 53
  • Drug‑resistance overlap: rpoB, gyrA, embB, pncA, gidB, rpoC, katG
  • Use case in the paper: primary scan, hypothesis generation
CRyPTIC consortium
5195 isolates

A globally diverse, internationally curated collection (Comprehensive Resistance Prediction for Tuberculosis: an International Consortium). Larger, more lineages, broader geography. MIC measurements for 13 antibiotics on every isolate.

  • Genes flagged with ω > 1: 173
  • Overlap with Moldova hits: ~36% (≈19 genes shared)
  • Use case in the paper: replication, broader sweep, drug‑specific subsets

Why two datasets?

The same way you wouldn't trust a GWAS hit that only replicated in one cohort, a selection signal that only appears in one isolate collection is suspect. Genes that show up in both have weathered different sampling biases, different lineage compositions, different drug‑use histories — they're the credible discoveries.

Why only ~36% overlap?
Three big reasons: (1) Moldova is dominated by Lineage 2 (Beijing) — selection signals there reflect Beijing‑specific adaptive landscape; (2) different drugs are used at different rates in different regions; (3) statistical power scales with sample size — CRyPTIC's 5195 isolates can see weaker signals invisible at n=2057. The overlap genes are the core M.tb adaptive genes, the non‑overlap are dataset‑specific or lower‑confidence.

Lineage structure matters

Schematic lineage composition. Beijing‑dominated Moldova will detect different adaptive signals than the more cosmopolitan CRyPTIC.

3. Three scales of selection

Why this section matters

This is the headline finding of Ioerger & Shatby 2025: using all three GenomegaMap scales together recovers far more real selection signal than any single scale alone. The same data, three views, three different answers.

The foundational paper introduced GenomegaMap's three model variants. The Ioerger lab's TB scans are what those variants buy you in practice. The same genome, run three different ways, tells three different stories.

How to read each scale

The Ioerger / Shatby workflow
Run all three. Use Constant to triage genes (~50–200 candidates). Use Independent to localize specific residues within those candidates. Use the sliding window to identify spatial regions / domains. Each view answers a different question; you don't pick one.

4. The false‑positive problem in big screens

Why this section matters

Test 4,400 genes and your hit list will include things that aren't really under selection. Knowing the two main failure modes (and how to filter them out) is the difference between a credible paper and an embarrassing one.

Heads up: Most selection scans you'll see in the wild over‑report. Many published "positively selected gene" lists from earlier methods are 30–50% false positives once size and lineage filters are applied. The principles in this section are why GenomegaMap‑era results are more trustworthy.

Run a Bayesian selection test on 4,400 genes and you'll get hits. The question is which are real. Two failure modes dominate:

Failure mode 1: tiny genes

A gene with 30 codons and a single nonsynonymous mutation can post a 95% CrI for ω that just barely excludes 1 — but the data is sparse and the prior is doing most of the work. These false positives correlate strongly with gene size.

The lesson: at short gene lengths the bars are huge — even a true ω of 1 frequently produces apparent hits. This is exactly why short genes need stricter thresholds, larger sample sizes, or are filtered out entirely.

Failure mode 2: lineage‑specific drift fixed in the sample

If your dataset is dominated by Lineage 2 and a few nonsynonymous mutations got fixed early in the L2 ancestor by drift, every L2 isolate carries them. The naive count looks like strong selection but the actual evolutionary event happened once. GenomegaMap's parent‑dependent mutation model + the Wilson 2020 paper's careful frequency weighting partially correct for this, but it's the reason replication across lineages and across datasets matters.

Heuristic the paper uses
Genes flagged in both Moldova and CRyPTIC, with ω > 1 supported across multiple codons (not just one), in genes > 100 codons long, ideally with biological plausibility (known target of drug, immune evasion, stress response) → high confidence. Anything failing two of those criteria is at best a hypothesis to revisit.

5. Drug resistance signatures

Why this section matters

These are the positive controls. We already know which genes carry resistance mutations — they were discovered the hard way (drug development + clinical sequencing) over decades. If GenomegaMap can rediscover them automatically, it works.

The cleanest, easiest‑to‑interpret signal in the M.tb selection scan: drug resistance genes light up with ω > 1, often spectacularly so. This is a sanity check that the method works — and because we know which residues confer resistance to which antibiotic, we can validate the method's spatial precision, not just its gene‑level accuracy.

Why pncA is a special case
PZA resistance is unusual because almost any mutation that breaks PncA function works — premature stops, frameshifts, missense across the whole gene. So pncA's signal in GenomegaMap is flat ω > 1 across the gene, not focal hotspots. This is genuinely diagnostic: focal hotspot vs whole‑gene flat distribution tell you which mode of resistance evolution is operating.

6. The RpoB RDR story (a worked example)

Why this section matters

This is the textbook story for selection analysis in TB. If you only remember one worked example from this whole site, make it this one — it shows the method working end‑to‑end at single‑codon resolution on a famous, biologically well‑understood gene.

The 81‑bp Rifampicin Resistance‑Determining Region (RDR) of rpoB is the classical example. ~95% of all rifampicin‑resistant clinical M.tb isolates worldwide carry one of a small handful of mutations within codons 426–452 of rpoB. If GenomegaMap can't pick out this region, it can't pick out anything.

RpoB ω profile (simulated; based on Moldova‑like data)
independent‑codon model

Posterior mean ω at each codon of RpoB (~1178 codons). The clear spike at codons ~430–450 corresponds to the RDR. A second weaker peak at ~488–496 corresponds to "Cluster II" RDR mutations. The rest of the gene is heavily purifying (ω ≈ 0.1) — RpoB is essential, and most of it is intolerant to change.

What this teaches you about the method

  1. Spatial precision is real. The peak is genuinely localized to the RDR — not smeared across the gene. The independent‑codon model has codon‑level resolution.
  2. Background matters. Most of the gene sits at ω ≈ 0.1, not ω ≈ 1. A flat prior anchored at 1 would inflate ω at the RDR. Hierarchical priors learn the gene‑wide distribution and compare each codon to that baseline.
  3. Compensatory signal. rpoC also lights up — but its peaks are at residues physically interacting with RpoB in the polymerase complex. That's how you tell compensatory from primary resistance.
RpoB anatomy with hot codons. S450L alone accounts for ~50% of all clinical rifampicin resistance.

7. Diabetes & comorbidity selection (Shatby thesis)

Why this section matters

This is the most likely topic for your summer project — Shatby's thesis is the lab's most recent fully worked comparative analysis, and it sets the template for asking "do pathogen genomes evolve differently in [host condition X] vs not?"

In plain English TB doesn't evolve in a vacuum — it evolves inside a person. If that person has diabetes, the lung environment is different (more sugar, more acid, more oxidative stress). Maybe TB picks up different mutations in diabetic hosts than in non‑diabetic hosts? Shatby compared 189+189 isolates and found yes, several specific genes look different. Three of them are uncharacterized — discovery targets for follow‑up.

Anthony Shatby's undergraduate thesis (the Shatby in "Ioerger & Shatby 2025") asks a different, sharper question: do M.tb genomes evolve differently in diabetic vs non‑diabetic hosts? Diabetes is the single largest comorbidity for active TB worldwide — patients with type‑2 diabetes are ~3× more likely to develop active TB and tend to fare worse on standard treatment. If TB is feeling a different selective regime inside a diabetic host, we should be able to read it from comparative GenomegaMap.

The experimental design
189 M.tb isolates from diabetic patients vs 189 from non‑diabetics, balanced across three countries (Indonesia, Mexico, Peru). Run GenomegaMap independently on each subset. Compare per‑codon ω profiles. Genes whose selection signal is different between subsets are candidates for diabetes‑specific adaptation.

The intellectual win
Three of the seven hits (Rv1490, Rv3104c, Rv3635) are conserved hypotheticals — genes with no known function. Selection analysis identifies them as biologically important before any biochemistry has been done. This is the single most attractive thing about an unbiased selection scan: it can prioritize a "known unknown" for experimental follow‑up.

Why ROS and hypoxia are the leading hypotheses

Diabetic granulomas are different in a few specific, measurable ways: higher reactive oxygen species (ROS) burden from neutrophils, more hypoxic cores, altered lipid composition (FFAs, cholesterol), and a metabolite milieu shifted toward glucose / lactate excess. The Shatby hits map cleanly onto adaptations to those stresses:

  • tal, lldD1: rerouted carbon metabolism for a high‑sugar / high‑lactate environment.
  • otsB2: trehalose system, both for osmotic protection and cell wall remodelling under stress.
  • katG and ahpC (broader literature): canonical ROS defenses — these don't always reach significance but are biologically expected.

8. Functional categories of selected genes

Why this section matters

Individual gene hits are stories. Categories are themes. Once you've seen the four big themes (drug response, cell wall, stress, PE/PPE), you'll recognize them in any new pathogen scan.

Pull back from individual genes and look at the categories. Across the Moldova + CRyPTIC scans, the top‑level functional bins of genes with ω > 1 cluster into a small number of themes:

Bars show approximate count of selected genes per Mycobrowser/TubercuList functional category, combined across both datasets after filtering for replication.

The four big themes

  1. Drug response and resistance. Direct targets (rpoB, gyrA, katG, embB, pncA), efflux pumps, compensatory partners (rpoC). Largest, easiest‑to‑interpret bin.
  2. Cell wall and lipid metabolism. M.tb has the most complex cell envelope of any pathogen — mycolic acids, sulfolipids, PDIM. Many ω > 1 genes sit here. Cell wall is the primary host‑pathogen interface, so it's under constant selection.
  3. Stress response. Hypoxia (DosR regulon), oxidative stress (KatG, AhpC), pH (Mbt iron acquisition). M.tb experiences a stress gradient inside the granuloma; selection there is constant.
  4. PE/PPE family. A weird M.tb‑specific gene family with hundreds of paralogs, thought to be involved in immune modulation / antigenic variation. Notoriously hard to align, notoriously polymorphic — so it shows up in selection scans both because it's genuinely fast‑evolving and because alignment artifacts inflate ω. Treat with caution.
Watch for alignment artifacts
PE/PPE genes are repetitive and highly polymorphic. If your alignment includes paralogs or misalignments, ω will look inflated. The standard remedy is to drop PE/PPE from the analysis or re‑align them with a paralog‑aware aligner before running GenomegaMap. The Wilson 2020 paper explicitly notes this caveat.

Why this section matters

GenomegaMap is just one rule in a 9‑step pipeline. To work in the lab you need to know the whole pipeline — what comes in (raw reads), what comes out (codon counts), and how the candidate gene list flows out the other end into Topics 3 and 4.

What you'd actually run

# Snakefile (sketch). Each rule is one step in the diagram above.

rule trim:
    input:  "raw/{sample}_{1,2}.fq.gz"
    output: "trim/{sample}_{1,2}.fq.gz"
    shell:  "fastp -i {input[0]} -I {input[1]} -o {output[0]} -O {output[1]}"

rule map_to_h37rv:
    input:  trim="trim/{sample}_1.fq.gz", ref="ref/H37Rv.fasta"
    output: "bam/{sample}.bam"
    shell:  "bwa mem {input.ref} {input.trim} | samtools sort -o {output}"

rule call_variants:
    input:  bam="bam/{sample}.bam", ref="ref/H37Rv.fasta"
    output: "vcf/{sample}.vcf.gz"
    shell:  "bcftools mpileup -f {input.ref} {input.bam} | bcftools call -mv -Oz -o {output}"

rule codon_align:
    input:  vcfs=expand("vcf/{sample}.vcf.gz", sample=SAMPLES),
            gff="ref/H37Rv.gff"
    output: "alignments/{gene}.codonaln.fasta"
    script: "scripts/build_codon_alignment.py"

rule genomegamap:
    input:  "alignments/{gene}.codonaln.fasta"
    output: "posterior/{gene}.{model}.tsv"
    shell:  "genomegamap --model {wildcards.model} -i {input} -o {output}"

# Constant model: one ω for the whole gene
genomegamap --model constant \
    -i alignments/rpoB.codonaln.fasta \
    -o posterior/rpoB.constant.tsv \
    --iters 50000 --burnin 10000 --thin 10 \
    --prior-mu 0.001 --prior-kappa 2.0

# Independent model: per‑codon ω, hierarchical Dirichlet prior
genomegamap --model independent \
    -i alignments/rpoB.codonaln.fasta \
    -o posterior/rpoB.independent.tsv \
    --iters 100000 --burnin 25000 --thin 25 \
    --hyperprior-alpha 1.0

# Sliding window: ~30 codon windows
genomegamap --model window \
    -i alignments/rpoB.codonaln.fasta \
    -o posterior/rpoB.window.tsv \
    --window-size 30 --window-step 1 \
    --iters 50000 --burnin 10000

import pandas as pd, numpy as np

# Load posterior samples for one gene/model
post = pd.read_table("posterior/rpoB.independent.tsv")
# columns: codon, sample_idx, omega

summary = (
    post.groupby("codon")["omega"]
        .agg(mean="mean",
             lo=lambda x: np.quantile(x, 0.025),
             hi=lambda x: np.quantile(x, 0.975),
             p_pos=lambda x: (x > 1).mean())
        .reset_index()
)

# A "hit" codon: posterior P(ω > 1) > 0.95
hits = summary[summary.p_pos > 0.95]
print(f"RpoB has {len(hits)} hot codons:")
print(hits[["codon", "mean", "lo", "hi", "p_pos"]])
