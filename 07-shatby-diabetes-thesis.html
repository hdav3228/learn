Paper 3 · Shatby 2026 · diabetes-specific selection

Comparative analysis of selected TB genes with diabetes comorbidity

This is the closest written blueprint for the summer project. It compares M. tuberculosis isolates from patients with diabetes against matched non-diabetes isolates, runs GenomegaMap separately on each group, and asks which genes show stronger positive selection in the diabetes host environment.

Cohorts Methods Main hits
In plain English GenomegaMap gives one omega distribution per gene per group. This thesis asks: if TB grows in patients with diabetes, do different genes look adaptively useful than in TB from patients without diabetes? The answer is tentative but important: several genes, especially tal, otsB2, and lldD1, repeatedly show up as diabetes-relevant candidates.

1. Why this paper matters

Why this section matters

GenomegaMap and the Ioerger/Shatby TB scans tell you the method. This thesis tells you the specific diabetes-vs-non-diabetes analysis pattern you are now trying to reproduce and scale.

Project-specific

It is about the exact same biological question: diabetes comorbidity and TB adaptation.

Faster-relevant

The thesis explicitly notes that much of the analysis used HPRC Faster.

Output-relevant

It shows how to compare diabetes and non-diabetes omega values after separate runs, setting the analytical baseline.

2. Core question

The central question is not simply "which genes have high dN/dS?" It is: which genes have stronger evidence of positive selection in diabetes isolates than in non-diabetes isolates?

Important distinction
A high diabetes omega value alone is not enough. A gene is more interesting for this project if diabetes omega is elevated relative to non-diabetes omega, because that suggests a diabetes-specific selective pressure instead of a general TB selective pressure.

4. Methods

Manual dN/dS

The thesis first uses manual mutation counting to rank genes by nonsynonymous and synonymous changes. This gives intuition and flags genes, but it is less rigorous than the Bayesian model.

GenomegaMap

GenomegaMap is run separately for diabetes and non-diabetes groups. Its MCMC output gives mean omega values and credible intervals for each gene in each group.

Δω = ωdiabetes - ωnon-diabetes

Because GenomegaMap models one population at a time, the thesis approximates the diabetes-minus-non-diabetes difference by resampling from both MCMC outputs. That gives an approximate distribution for ωdiabetes - ωnon-diabetes, plus a probability like P(diabetes > non-diabetes).

5. GenomegaMap results & Figures

The main result pattern

Several genes show elevated diabetes omega relative to non-diabetes. The strongest named candidates in the thesis are not all "statistically final" in the same way, but they repeatedly appear across rankings and biological interpretation.

Understanding the Results Figures
When looking at the $\Delta\omega$ distribution charts in the thesis: if the bulk of the histogram sits to the right of zero, it means that during MCMC sampling, the $\omega$ drawn for the diabetes group was almost always larger than the $\omega$ drawn for the non-diabetes group.

6. Gene stories & Interpretation Example

tal + otsB2

These are the clearest biological story pair. tal sits in the pentose phosphate pathway, while otsB2 is tied to trehalose synthesis. Together they point toward metabolism, stress survival, and cell-wall-related biology under diabetes-linked host pressure.

lldD1

lldD1 is especially important because it appears in manual and GenomegaMap rankings and has a diabetes omega interval above 1. The thesis connects it to hypoxia, which is plausible in diabetes-associated host environments.

Worked Interpretation Example: tal (Rv1448c)
The Data: GenomegaMap says $\omega$ in diabetes is 2.59 (CrI: 1.2-4.1) and $\omega$ in non-diabetes is 0.70 (CrI: 0.3-1.1).
The Interpretation: Because the non-diabetes interval straddles 1 (mostly below it) while the diabetes interval is entirely above 1, we conclude `tal` is evolving neutrally/purifyingly in normal hosts, but under *active positive selection* in diabetic hosts. This points to the hyperglycemic environment of a diabetic patient driving mutations in the pentose phosphate pathway.

7. Limitations & What not to overclaim

What NOT to overclaim
Do not claim that these genes *cause* diabetes, or that they make TB entirely incurable in diabetic patients. Claim only that the selective landscape acting on these proteins differs depending on the host's metabolic state.
  • Sample size: 189 diabetes and 189 non-diabetes isolates is useful, but still small compared with the number of public TB genomes.
  • Metadata scarcity: many public genomes do not have diabetes status, so cohort construction is a bottleneck.
  • Separate GenomegaMap runs: direct diabetes-vs-non-diabetes difference modeling is approximated from MCMC samples, not native to GenomegaMap.
  • Lineage and resistance confounding: differences in selection could reflect lineage, drug resistance, or sampling structure unless controlled carefully.

8. How to use it now

What to remember
The overarching pipeline here is what we are trying to scale. We need to parse metadata to get a clean cohort, run GenomegaMap in parallel, and then statistically compare the posteriors.
  1. Use this paper to understand the diabetes-vs-non-diabetes comparison logic.
  2. Use the current workbook and good-only sample lists for the actual isolate set.
  3. Use Faster only after the GenomegaMap/gcat environment passes a tiny one-gene test.
  4. When reviewing output, look for genes with elevated diabetes omega, credible intervals, and useful biological interpretation.
