Open FASTA and vocabulary decoderReference

One-page decoder

TB gene
A protein-coding stretch of the M. tuberculosis genome. You run GenomegaMap gene by gene, not on the whole genome as one giant sequence.
Isolate
A TB sample from one patient. If a gene file has many isolate sequences, it means the same gene was collected from many patients' TB genomes.
FASTA file
A plain-text sequence file. Each sequence starts with a header line beginning with >, followed by DNA letters like A, C, G, and T. People often say "fasta file," not "fast file."
Aligned FASTA
A FASTA file where all sequences have been lined up to the same coordinate system. Hyphens, -, are gaps or padding, not DNA bases. They keep every isolate aligned position-by-position.
FASTA vs FASTQ
FASTA stores cleaned/assembled sequences and uses > headers. FASTQ stores raw sequencing reads plus quality scores and usually uses @ headers. Your pasted file is FASTA, not FASTQ.
SRR accession
An identifier for a sequencing run/sample from the Sequence Read Archive. In your pasted file, headers like >SRR10809201 are isolate/sample IDs.
Cohort
A group of samples chosen by a shared label. Here, one cohort is TB isolates from patients with diabetes, and the other is TB isolates from patients without diabetes.
Constant model
The GenomegaMap mode that gives one ω estimate for the whole gene. It does not tell you which exact codon is selected; it answers whether the gene overall has selection signal.
Sampled posterior distribution
A collection of many MCMC-sampled parameter values. If GenomegaMap saves 2000 ω samples, their histogram is the model's uncertainty about ω for that gene.
Faster
The HPRC supercomputer Dr. Ioerger wants you to use because thousands of gene runs are too slow on one desktop.

Open codon-level dN/dS primerBio basics

dN/dS and ω from zero

1
Genes are read in codons. A codon is a 3-letter DNA word, like GAA or GAG, that tells the cell which amino acid to put into a protein.
2
Some DNA mutations are silent. If a codon changes but the amino acid stays the same, that is a synonymous mutation. Example: GAA and GAG both code for glutamate, so that change does not alter the protein.
3
Some DNA mutations change the protein. If a codon change swaps one amino acid for another, that is a nonsynonymous mutation. Those are the ones natural selection can strongly favor or remove, because the protein itself changed.
4
dN/dS compares those two rates. dN means the rate of nonsynonymous change. dS means the rate of synonymous change. ω is just another name for dN/dS.
Open ω interpretation and model modesStats + models
ω valueMeaningPlain-English interpretation
ω < 1 Purifying selection Protein-changing mutations are being removed.
ω = 1 Neutral-ish evolution Protein-changing mutations are not obviously favored or removed.
ω > 1 Positive selection Protein-changing mutations appear more often than expected.
Why not just count mutations?
Raw mutation counts are biased because most random codon changes alter the amino acid. dN/dS corrects for possible silent vs protein-changing sites.

Open Bayesian and MCMC refresherStats depth

Bayesian inference without panic

In plain English Bayesian inference means: start with possible parameter values, ask which values make the observed data likely, then update your beliefs into a posterior distribution.
posterior ∝ likelihood × prior
Parameter
An unknown number the model is trying to estimate. Here the important one is ω, but GenomegaMap also estimates κ and θ.
Prior
What values the model thinks are reasonable before seeing this gene's data. It should be broad enough that the data can still speak.
Likelihood
A score for how well a proposed parameter value explains the observed gene sequences. If ω = 2 makes the observed codon changes more plausible than ω = 0.2, then ω = 2 has higher likelihood.
Posterior
The updated distribution after combining prior and likelihood. This is the answer GenomegaMap is trying to approximate.
Posterior mean
The average of the sampled posterior values. Useful as a summary, but not enough by itself.
Credible interval
A range containing most of the posterior probability, often 95%. If the interval crosses 1, the evidence for positive selection is weaker even if the mean is above 1.

What the Bayesian model is doing for one gene

1
Data: many TB isolates have the same gene sequence, with occasional mutations.
2
Question: what ω value would make this pattern of synonymous and nonsynonymous mutations plausible?
3
Output: not one answer, but a distribution of possible ω values. If the distribution is mostly below 1, think purifying selection. If it is mostly above 1, think positive selection.

MCMC: why the output has lots of rows

The problem

The posterior distribution is too complicated to calculate exactly. GenomegaMap cannot simply write down a neat formula and finish.

The trick

MCMC walks around parameter space and saves many plausible values. The saved values behave like samples from the posterior distribution.

  1. Start somewhere. Pick initial values for ω, κ, and θ.
  2. Propose a small change. Try a nearby value, like changing ω from 0.90 to 0.95.
  3. Score the new value. Use the likelihood to ask whether the new value explains the sequence data better.
  4. Accept or reject. Better proposals usually get accepted. Worse proposals can still sometimes be accepted so the chain does not get stuck.
  5. Repeat many times. The saved values form a histogram. That histogram is the posterior approximation.
Why repeated rows happen
In an MCMC output file, a parameter may stay exactly the same from one saved row to the next. That usually means a proposed move was rejected, so the chain kept the old value.

4. Biology and cohorts

Why the biology matters

GenomegaMap will give you numbers, but the project is about interpreting those numbers as evolutionary pressure inside real TB patient populations.

The starting comparison
diabetes

The first project compares TB isolates from patients with diabetes against isolates from patients without diabetes. The hypothesis is that diabetes creates a different host environment, so mutations in some TB genes may become more advantageous in that group.

  • Hyperglycemia can change nutrient and stress conditions.
  • Immune and inflammatory responses can differ.
  • Oxidative stress may reshape which bacterial functions are beneficial.
  • The dataset may be too small to detect weak effects, so uncertainty matters.
Later comparisons
extensions

After the diabetes analysis, the same machinery can be rerun by regrouping the same or related sequences in biologically meaningful ways.

  • Drug resistant vs non-drug resistant isolates.
  • Lineage 2 vs Lineage 4, and eventually L2/L3/L4 comparisons.
  • Genes uniquely selected in one lineage or host condition.
  • Agreement or disagreement with Anthony Shatby's thesis results.

Key TB terms from the conversation

Clinical isolate
A TB sample taken from a real patient and sequenced as a genome.
TBProfiler
A tool that reads TB sequencing data and reports lineage plus genetic markers of drug resistance.
Genotypic resistance
Resistance inferred from known mutations in the genome. This differs from phenotypic resistance, where the isolate is experimentally tested against a drug.
Lineage
A major branch of the M. tuberculosis family tree. Lineage composition can affect selection signals, so it must be tracked.
MTBC animal-associated species
The broader M. tuberculosis complex includes close relatives such as M. bovis in cattle, M. pinnipedii in seals, and M. microti in small mammals. These came up as context for ancient or animal-associated lineages.
Confounding risk
A gene can look diabetes-associated if the diabetes and non-diabetes groups have different lineage mixtures or drug-resistance mixtures. Keep asking: is this signal about diabetes, lineage, resistance, geography, or sampling?

5. GenomegaMap mechanics

The statistical core

GenomegaMap estimates a posterior distribution over selection parameters. In the constant model, the transcript highlighted three fitted parameters: ω, κ, and θ.

What ω tells you

ω is dN/dS. If it is below 1, nonsynonymous changes are being filtered out by purifying selection. If it is around 1, the gene is roughly neutral. If it is above 1, nonsynonymous changes are accumulating faster than expected, suggesting positive selection.

What the posterior adds

You do not just get one ω. You get many sampled ω values from MCMC. Their histogram is an empirical approximation of uncertainty about ω after seeing the data.

ParameterPlain-English meaningMeeting-level interpretation
ω dN/dS, the selection parameter. The main biological output: purifying, neutral, or positive selection.
κ Transition/transversion rate ratio. Controls mutation bias: some base changes happen more often than others.
θ Population-scaled mutation/diversity parameter. A nuisance but important parameter fit jointly with ω and κ.
log-likelihood How compatible the data are with the model at the current parameter values. Useful for trace plots and diagnosing whether the chain has stabilized.

Reading the constant-model output

1
Rows are saved MCMC samples. The example in the meeting saved every fifth iteration out of 10,000 iterations, so the output table is a thinned trace.
2
Columns are current parameter values. Some rows repeat because a proposed parameter move was rejected, so the chain stayed at the old value.
3
The histogram is the posterior approximation. If sampled ω values cluster around 0.9 with occasional values above 1.5, the mean may be near neutral or slightly purifying, but uncertainty still reaches into positive-selection territory.

Use this first. One ω is fit for the whole gene. This is the right first pass for ~4000 genes because it is simpler, faster, and directly supports a gene-level diabetes vs non-diabetes comparison.

One ω is fit for each codon. This can identify exact residues under selection, but each codon has less information, so the results are noisier and more vulnerable to false positives.

A local region shares an ω, and the window moves or is inferred across the sequence. This is good for hotspots, but it is not the immediate first assignment from this meeting.

The one-sentence version
GenomegaMap uses MCMC to sample parameter values whose likelihood under the codon model is high, and the cloud of accepted samples becomes the posterior distribution you interpret.

7. Comparing cohorts

What happens after the runs finish

GenomegaMap gives one posterior distribution per gene per cohort. The science question is whether those distributions differ enough to suggest condition-specific selection.

Easy ad hoc comparison

Run GenomegaMap separately in each group, then compare the sampled ω distributions. You can compare means, overlaps, a sampled difference distribution, or the probability that ωdiabetes is greater than ωnon-diabetes.

More principled future model

Dr. Ioerger's C reimplementation may eventually let the model fit separate group parameters jointly. That would be a cleaner Bayesian comparison than two separate runs plus post-hoc overlap tests.

GeneDiabetes posteriorNon-diabetes posteriorMeeting interpretation
Gene A Mean ω = 1.35, wide interval Mean ω = 0.80, wide interval Suggestive, but overlap may be too large to claim a difference.
Gene B Mean ω = 1.80, most samples > 1 Mean ω = 0.45, most samples < 1 Candidate diabetes-associated selection signal.
Gene C Mean ω = 0.35 Mean ω = 0.32 Both groups show purifying selection; probably not a diabetes-specific hit.

How to think about posterior overlap

1
Sample one ω value from the diabetes posterior and one from the non-diabetes posterior.
2
Subtract them to get one sampled difference, Δω = ωdiabetes - ωnon-diabetes.
3
Repeat many times. If nearly all sampled differences are positive, the diabetes group likely has higher selection for that gene. If the difference distribution crosses zero heavily, be cautious.
Multiple testing
Thousands of genes means some will look interesting by chance. A final analysis needs ranking, false-discovery control, biological validation, and checks for lineage/drug-resistance confounding.

Questions for Dr. Ioerger

Arrive with specifics

These are the highest-value questions to bring to the next meeting. They turn vague confusion into concrete decisions.

Best way to ask
Ask for decisions, not lectures. The useful question shape is: "I have this exact input path, this XML field, and this proposed output summary. Is this the production setting you want?"
  1. Which exact input files are authoritative? This means: which files should be treated as the real source of truth? You do not want to accidentally run an old copy, a test example, or a partially filtered dataset.
  2. What output columns should I preserve? This means: when GenomegaMap writes a table, which columns matter for analysis? At minimum, you care about iteration/sample number, log-likelihood, ω, κ, θ, model type, gene name, and cohort.
  3. How many MCMC iterations should the real run use? This means: how long should each chain run? More iterations usually give a better posterior approximation, but they cost more cluster time.
  4. What burn-in and thinning should I use? Burn-in is the early part of the chain you throw away because it may not have settled yet. Thinning means saving every nth sample instead of every sample.
  5. What is the approved Slurm template? This means: ask for the exact job-array script pattern Dr. Ioerger trusts, including memory, time limit, output folders, and how the array index chooses a gene.
  6. Should I run/test on Galaxy first, Faster first, or both? This means: separate "known install recipe" from "production cluster run" so you do not debug installation and Slurm scaling at the same time.
  7. Should the XML paths be relative to Ioerger's Lab, absolute on Faster, or copied into scratch? This means: prevent jobs from silently failing because the XML resolves paths from the wrong working directory.
  8. How do we adjust for lineage and drug resistance? This means: check that a diabetes signal is not secretly caused by the diabetes group having different TB lineages or more drug-resistant isolates.
  9. What counts as a hit? A hit is a gene worth following up. Ask whether to rank genes by mean ω difference, posterior probability, credible interval separation, low overlap, or a combined score.

Mini glossary

Purifying selection
Selection removing amino-acid-changing mutations; ω < 1.
Positive selection
Selection favoring amino-acid-changing mutations; ω > 1.
Posterior distribution
The probability distribution over parameters after combining prior assumptions, the model, and observed data.
Likelihood
The probability of the observed data under a model and a specific parameter setting.
Burn-in
Early MCMC samples discarded because the chain may not yet represent the target posterior.
Thinning
Saving every nth MCMC sample to reduce file size and autocorrelation.
Service units
HPC compute allocation currency. A bad large run can waste them quickly.
