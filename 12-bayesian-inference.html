Liu & Lawrence 1999 · Foundational Math

Bayesian Inference on Biopolymer Models

Before GenomegaMap could use MCMC to sample ω across a genome, the computational biology field needed a formal way to handle uncertainty in sequence models. This 1999 paper is the mathematical ancestor to our entire approach.

Plain English Methods Results
Read this first — in plain English Instead of saying "the best alignment is exactly this one," or "the mutation rate is exactly 1.5," Bayesian statistics gives you a probability distribution of all possible answers. This paper showed how to use math tricks (like Dynamic Programming) and computational tricks (like MCMC/Gibbs sampling) to actually calculate those distributions for biology problems. It's the reason GenomegaMap gives us a 95% Credible Interval for selection, rather than just a single, fragile point estimate.

1. What this paper is saying

The core message

Most early bioinformatics tools gave point estimates (a single best answer). This paper argues that we should use Bayesian inference to calculate posterior distributions for all unknowns, providing built-in uncertainty estimates.

The authors demonstrate that you can take classical recursive dynamic programming algorithms (used for sequence alignment or segmentation) and modify them to perform the massive sums/integrals required by Bayesian statistics. When exact math isn't possible, they introduce Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques like Gibbs sampling to approximate the answer.

2. Why this matters for our project

Our entire workflow relies on GenomegaMap, which uses a Bayesian hidden Markov model and MCMC sampling to infer the ω (dN/dS) ratio. To trust GenomegaMap, you need to understand why it produces a credible interval rather than a single p-value.

🎲

The MCMC Engine

This paper explains Gibbs sampling, the ancestor of the MCMC process GenomegaMap uses to explore different ω values.

📊

Posterior Distributions

We don't get one ω. We get a distribution of ωs. This paper explains why that is mathematically superior.

Worked Interpretation: Point Estimates vs. Distributions

1
The scenario: You run a traditional tool on a very short TB gene (e.g., a 150bp ribosomal protein) with only one mutation.
2
The old way: A traditional Maximum Likelihood tool sees 1 nonsynonymous mutation and 0 synonymous mutations. It calculates dN/dS = 1/0 = Infinity. It reports a point estimate of ω = ∞. This is practically useless and statistically unstable.
3
The Bayesian way (this paper): A Bayesian tool (like GenomegaMap) sees the same data. But it integrates that weak data with a prior distribution. Because there's very little data, the posterior distribution stays very wide. It might say the mean ω is 1.5, with a 95% credible interval of [0.1, 8.0]. This honestly reflects the massive uncertainty rather than confidently reporting nonsense.

3. Methods decoder: Section-by-section breakdown

Basic Bayesian Statistics

The paper defines the posterior as proportional to the Likelihood × Prior. It introduces the concept of a conjugate prior (like a Beta distribution for coin flips) which makes the math easy because the posterior ends up being the same shape as the prior.

Missing Data Framework

In bioinformatics, we often have missing data (e.g., we don't know the exact evolutionary path or the exact alignment). Bayesian methods treat missing data just like unknown parameters: we integrate (sum) over all possibilities to get the "marginal likelihood."

A Coin Example & Sequence Segmentation

They use a two-coin game as an analogy for sequence segmentation. If a sequence has two regions with different properties (like the "sliding window" in GenomegaMap), we don't know where the boundary is. We can sum over all possible boundary points using dynamic programming to find the posterior distribution of the boundary location.

4. Key results explained

Result 1: Exact Bayesian Segmentation
Using an adapted dynamic programming algorithm, they exactly calculated the posterior probability of change points in the yeast histone H1 genome sequence. This proved that Bayesian methods were computationally feasible for finding structural boundaries in biological sequences.
Result 2: Bayesian Alignment
They showed that instead of fixing gap penalties (λo, λe) and looking for the "best" alignment, you can put priors on the gap penalties, sum over all alignments, and get a posterior distribution for the gap penalties themselves. The data "tells" you what the gap penalties should have been!

Worked Interpretation: The sliding window

1
The GenomegaMap connection: GenomegaMap doesn't assume a gene has a single ω rate. It allows the rate to change across the sequence, using a sliding window.
2
The Bayesian link: This is directly descended from the "Sequence Segmentation" result in this paper. Instead of hard-coding where the selection boundaries are, GenomegaMap uses MCMC to sum over all possible window boundary placements, giving us a smooth curve of posterior selection probability across the gene.

5. Talk track

What to say in a meeting

"When interpreting our GenomegaMap results, we need to lean heavily on the Bayesian foundation described by Liu and Lawrence. If a gene has a high posterior mean ω but an extremely wide credible interval, we know the MCMC sampler struggled to converge or the sequence lacked enough SNPs to overcome the prior. The Bayesian approach protects us from overinterpreting noisy, short genes—a trap that traditional maximum-likelihood point estimators fall into."

6. What not to overclaim

What NOT to overclaim
  • Bayesian != Perfect: Bayesian inference does not magically create data where none exists. If a sequence has no mutations, the posterior distribution will simply match the prior. The method handles uncertainty perfectly, but it cannot invent certainty.
  • Priors matter: The results are still dependent on the choice of prior. A very strong, incorrect prior can overwhelm weak data. (This is why GenomegaMap's default priors are designed to be relatively uninformative/diffuse).
