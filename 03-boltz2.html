Boltz‑2: structure + binding affinity in one model

AlphaFold made a graduate course's worth of crystallography optional. Boltz‑2 goes the next step — predicting how strongly a small molecule binds a target alongside the 3D complex, in ~20 seconds on one GPU, at accuracy that approaches the gold‑standard free‑energy perturbation methods at roughly 1000× the speed.

1. Why structure prediction matters

Why this section matters

Drug design needs 3D shapes. For decades, getting one took 6–24 months of crystallography. AlphaFold and friends shrank that to seconds. This section sets up why we care about Boltz‑2 in particular among the predictors.

Once you have a list of genes under positive selection — say, the 53 genes from the Ioerger lab's TB scan paper — the next question is always the same: can we drug it? Drugs work by physically docking into a pocket on a protein. Without a 3D model you can't reason about what ligand will fit, you can't run virtual screening, and you can't rationally design analogues. For a long time the answer was "wait for X‑ray crystallography to catch up," which for any one new target meant 6–24 months and ~$100 k.

Deep learning broke that bottleneck. In 2020, AlphaFold2 demonstrated near‑experimental accuracy on single‑chain proteins. AlphaFold3 (2024) added DNA, RNA, ligands, ions and modifications. Boltz‑1 and Boltz‑2 are the openly released MIT‑licensed analogues — Boltz‑2 is the first to predict binding affinity jointly with structure, which is the input metric a medicinal chemist actually cares about.

In one sentence
Boltz‑2 takes (protein sequence + small molecule SMILES) → (3D complex + log10 IC50), trained end‑to‑end on the PDB plus curated PubChem/ChEMBL affinities plus molecular dynamics trajectories.

2. AlphaFold2 — the baseline you have to know

Why this section matters

Every modern structure predictor (AF3, Boltz‑1, Boltz‑2, ESM‑Fold) is a delta from AF2. Knowing AF2's five blocks lets you read any of those papers in 20 minutes instead of a week.

In plain English AlphaFold2 takes a protein sequence (a string of amino acid letters) and predicts the 3D coordinates of every atom. The trick: it also looks at thousands of related sequences from other species. If two positions in the protein always mutate together across species, they're physically touching in 3D — that's the signal AF2 squeezes out. Then it predicts which atoms are close to which, and finally fills in 3D coordinates consistent with those predictions.

Boltz‑2 is best understood as the third step of a family tree: AlphaFold2 → AlphaFold3 → Boltz‑{1, 2}. Skipping the lineage will leave architectural decisions feeling arbitrary, so here's the AF2 picture in one diagram.

Figure 1. AlphaFold2 in five blocks. The architectural innovations are all in the Evoformer: it lets information flow back and forth between coevolutionary signal in the MSA and pair‑wise contact predictions, repeatedly.

The MSA, why it's there, and what it does

A multiple sequence alignment of the target sequence with hundreds or thousands of homologs from related organisms exposes the only signal we have for which residues are important: covariation. If two residues mutate together across species, they're almost certainly in physical contact, because mutating one without compensating the other would break the structure. AlphaFold2's row attention reads each MSA sequence; column attention reads each position across sequences; together they extract the covariation pattern as a pair representation before geometry is ever assigned.

The Evoformer in plain words

Two parallel "towers" of transformer blocks: one operates on the MSA tensor (N × L × c), the other on the pair tensor (L × L × c′). They communicate through three operations:

  • MSA → pair (outer product mean): feed coevolutionary signal into the L×L grid.
  • Pair → MSA (bias on row attention): feed the geometric model back into how MSA residues attend to each other.
  • Triangle attention/multiplication: the pair grid is updated under the constraint that the geometry must be self‑consistent — if i is close to j, and j is close to k, then i should be near k (the triangle inequality).

The structure module

After 48 Evoformer blocks, AF2 turns the final pair + MSA representation into 3D coordinates via Invariant Point Attention (IPA), a custom attention mechanism that operates on rigid local frames (one per residue). It's invariant under global rotation/translation, so the network never has to learn that "rotating the protein doesn't change the protein." The whole structure is decoded in one pass; recycling refines it.

3. AlphaFold3 and the diffusion turn

Why this section matters

AF2 was protein‑only. Drug discovery needs proteins plus small molecules (ligands), ions, RNA, DNA. AF3 is the upgrade that handles all of those — and the way it does it (tokens + diffusion) is exactly what Boltz‑2 inherits.

AF2 only handles proteins (with limited support for templates). To predict a complex with a small molecule, an ion, or a modified residue, you needed a different tool. AlphaFold3 (Abramson et al., 2024) generalizes the architecture in two big ways:

  • Token‑based representation. Instead of "residues," AF3 uses generic tokens. A standard amino acid is one token; a heavy atom of a small molecule is one token; a nucleic acid base is one token. The MSA is dropped (or shrunk) for non‑protein tokens.
  • Diffusion structure module. The IPA decoder is replaced by a denoising diffusion module. Coordinates start as Gaussian noise and are denoised iteratively, conditioned on the trunk's pair representation. This lets the model handle arbitrary atoms and lets uncertainty in conformation come out naturally as sample diversity.
A licensing aside
AlphaFold3's weights are released under a Creative Commons license that excludes commercial use. That, plus the urgency of letting industry use these tools, is the main reason the Boltz family exists — it's an MIT‑licensed reproduction of the AF3 architecture from the MIT CSAIL group, free to build on commercially.

4. Boltz‑1 — the open replication (Wohlwend et al. 2024)

Why this section matters

AF3's weights forbid commercial use, which slowed adoption. Boltz‑1 is the MIT‑licensed reimplementation that does let you use it commercially — and it's the predecessor whose deltas to Boltz‑2 are what you'll see in the paper.

Boltz‑1 essentially reproduced AF3 but with the goal of being fully open and pushing efficiency. The architecture is broadly the same — pair representation refined by a PairFormer (a renaming of the Evoformer with the column‑attention removed), then a diffusion structure module on tokens — but with several deliberate differences:

TemplatesExcluded in v1 (added in v2)Included

Boltz‑1 also introduced Boltz‑Steering: a Feynman‑Kac style guidance that biases the diffusion process at inference time toward chemically valid structures (no atom clashes, correct chirality, correct bond lengths) without retraining. It uses a sequential‑Monte‑Carlo resampling: spawn many particles per timestep, weight them by an energy potential, resample. The result was Boltz‑1x, which passes ~97% of physical sanity checks vs. ~57% for the un‑steered base model.

5. Boltz‑2 — the version you'll actually use

Why this section matters

This is the tool the lab runs day‑to‑day. This section explains the three additions over Boltz‑1: affinity head, controllability, and a deeper trunk.

In plain English Boltz‑1 said: "Here's the protein–ligand 3D shape." Boltz‑2 adds: "...and here's also a number telling you how strongly the ligand binds." That second number (binding affinity) is what medicinal chemists use to rank drug candidates. Predicting it directly with the structure means one tool replaces a slow, expensive lab assay.

Boltz‑2 (Passaro, Corso, Wohlwend et al., 2025) takes Boltz‑1 and adds three things:

  1. An affinity prediction head trained on PubChem and ChEMBL — this is the headline feature.
  2. Controllability: the user can specify experimental conditions (X‑ray vs NMR vs MD), pocket constraints, and contact constraints, and the diffusion is steered accordingly.
  3. Trunk scaling: 64 PairFormer layers (up from 48), plus templates similar to AF3, plus bf16 throughout for memory savings.
Figure 2. Boltz‑2 architecture. The trunk is shared with Boltz‑1's design but deeper. The diffusion module produces atomic coordinates, and the affinity head is a separate PairFormer that consumes the structure to predict P(binder) and log10(IC50).

Versus Boltz‑1 in one paragraph

PairFormer depth doubled (48→64), templates added, Boltz‑Steering integrated by default rather than as a separate run, training data expanded with NMR ensembles and molecular dynamics trajectories so the model has seen multiple conformers per sequence, and — the headline — an affinity head trained on a curated subset of PubChem and ChEMBL with Ki, Kd, and IC50 measurements all standardized to log10(IC50) in μM.

6. The PairFormer trunk in detail

Why this section matters · optional deep dive

This section is deeper architecture detail. If you just want to use Boltz‑2, skim and jump to §10 (Running it). If you want to modify or fine‑tune it, read carefully — this is the heart of the model.

The "trunk" is the part of the model whose job is to take inputs (sequence, MSA, templates, ligand graph) and produce a refined pair representation zij ∈ ℝc for every ordered pair of tokens (i, j) and a single representation si ∈ ℝc per token. After 64 PairFormer blocks z encodes predicted distances, orientations, contacts, and chemistry — everything the diffusion module needs to draw 3D coordinates.

Tokens, not residues

An AlphaFold2 input is a sequence of L amino acids. A Boltz‑2 input is a sequence of L tokens:

  • One token per amino acid (20 standard + modifications),
  • One token per nucleic acid base (DNA/RNA),
  • One token per heavy atom in any small molecule, ion, or non‑standard residue.

This is what lets a single network handle, say, a 280‑residue kinase + an ATP analog + a Mg2+ ion + a peptide substrate as one cohesive complex. The downside is that small molecules can balloon the token count — a 60‑heavy‑atom ligand adds 60 tokens, and the pair representation grows quadratically. PairFormer's triangle attention is O(L³) per layer in the naïve implementation; the Boltz authors implemented a custom Triton kernel ("trifast") that reduces the memory cost to O(L²).

Triangle multiplication and triangle attention

Triangle operations enforce self‑consistency of the pair representation. Concretely, if zij represents an edge from i to j and zjk an edge from j to k, then triangle multiplication updates zik using a function of zij and zjk over all intermediate vertices k:

ziknew ← g(Σj aij ⊙ bjk)

where a, b are linear projections of z and g is a gated layer. There are four flavors — outgoing/incoming × multiplication/attention — each of which lets the network express different types of geometric reasoning. The intuition is just the triangle inequality: if i–j and j–k are short edges, the i–k distance is constrained.

MSA module

The MSA module is reduced relative to AF2: most blocks now sit in the pair representation. MSA is processed once early, projected into the pair grid via outer‑product mean, and discarded. This is a deliberate tradeoff — AF2 spent enormous compute on MSA cross‑talk that AF3 / Boltz showed was not necessary if the pair stack is deep enough.

7. The diffusion module — making structure from noise

Why this section matters

The trunk gave us relationships between residues. This section is how those relationships become actual 3D coordinates. The trick — borrowed from image generators like Stable Diffusion — is to start from random noise and slowly "sharpen" it into a structure.

In plain English Imagine a sculptor with a block of marble — except the marble is a cloud of random points. At each step, the model nudges every atom slightly toward where it "should" be, given the trunk's hints. After 200 nudges, what was noise is a protein. The advantage over older methods (AF2's IPA): it's the same algorithm whether you're folding a backbone, placing a side chain, or docking a ligand. Everything is just "atoms in 3D" to a diffusion model.

After 64 trunk blocks you have a fixed pair representation z and a single representation s. These condition a generative model that produces 3D coordinates. The model is a denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM); coordinates start as Gaussian noise and are denoised toward a likely structure.

The forward (training) process

At training time you take a known PDB structure x0 (a tensor of all heavy‑atom positions in ℝ3) and add Gaussian noise at level σt drawn from a noise schedule:

xt = x0 + σt · ε,   ε ∼ 𝒩(0, I)

The network is trained to predict the original x0 given xt, t, s, z. Loss is weighted MSE in coordinate space:

ℒdiff = 𝔼t,ε [ w(t) · ‖ fθ(xt; t, s, z) − x0 ‖² ]

where w(t) follows the EDM weighting (t² + σdata²)/(t · σdata)² (Karras et al. 2022). This is a slight departure from AF3's weighting; the Boltz team found it more stable in low‑data regimes.

The reverse (sampling) process

At inference time you start with xT ~ 𝒩(0, σmax2I) and iterate:

  1. Predict the clean structure: x̂0 = fθ(xt; t, s, z).
  2. Take a small Euler step toward x̂0: xt−1 = xt + Δt · (x̂0 − xt) / σt.
  3. Optionally add a small new noise (SDE flavor; the deterministic ODE flavor doesn't).
  4. (Boltz‑specific) Apply Kabsch alignment between x̂0 and xt — a rigid‑body optimal rotation — before stepping. This reduces variance because the loss is invariant to global rotation of x̂0, so without alignment the network spends capacity guessing orientation.
  5. (Boltz‑specific) Apply Boltz‑Steering potentials: a clash penalty, a chirality penalty, optional pocket distance constraints, optional contact constraints. The gradient of each potential is added to the velocity field. With multiple particles, low‑weight ones are resampled (Sequential Monte Carlo).
  6. Repeat until t = 0. Output x0.

Real diffusion in Boltz‑2 has 200 timesteps default and operates in 3·N‑dimensional space, but the dynamics are exactly this: noise → small step toward predicted clean state → less noise → repeat.

Why diffusion instead of IPA?

Three reasons:

  • Atomic resolution out of the box. IPA built a backbone first then placed side chains. A small molecule has no backbone — diffusion gives all‑atom coordinates uniformly.
  • Sampling diversity. Sampling 5 times gives 5 plausibly different conformations rather than 5 perturbations of one answer. For a flexible loop or a flexible ligand, that's exactly the question we want answered.
  • Steerability. Diffusion's iterative sampler is the natural place to inject side information: classifier guidance, energy potentials, contact constraints. AF2's IPA has no analogous hook.

8. The affinity head — the new piece in Boltz‑2

Why this section matters

This is the reason Boltz‑2 made headlines. AF3 / Boltz‑1 told you the shape of a complex — useful, but doesn't directly answer "will my drug bind?". Boltz‑2 adds a head that predicts how strongly. For drug discovery work in the Ioerger lab, this is the most actionable piece of the whole pipeline.

In plain English Two predictors share the same backbone. The binary classifier is the cheap question: "is this compound a binder, yes or no?" — perfect for screening millions of molecules. The regressor is the expensive question: "how tightly does it bind, in nanomolar?" — used on the few hundred survivors of the cheap screen. Same idea as triage in an emergency room: fast screen first, careful measurement after.

After the diffusion module produces a structure x0 (and the trunk produced z), Boltz‑2 feeds them into a second PairFormer that focuses exclusively on protein–ligand and ligand–ligand atomic pairs. This module's job is two predictions:

Binary classifier — affinity_probability_binary

Sigmoid output ∈ [0, 1]. Trained on a curated set of binders/non‑binders. Used in the virtual screening regime: dock 1M compounds, keep the top‑scoring 1k, give those to the regressor.

Regressor — affinity_pred_value

Outputs log10(IC50/μM). Trained on Ki, Kd, and IC50 measurements all converted to a common log‑μM axis. Use this for lead optimization where you care about the difference between 10 nM and 1 nM.

The training data — "we curated the heck out of PubChem"

The Boltz‑2 paper spends most of its data section explaining curation. Raw bioactivity tables are a dumpster fire — the same compound can have IC50 measurements spanning four orders of magnitude across labs because of differences in assay format, target construct, ATP concentration, etc. To make a usable training set the authors:

  • Filtered for assays where the target sequence is unambiguously specified.
  • Aggregated multiple measurements per (target, ligand) pair using the geometric mean.
  • Standardized Ki, Kd, IC50 via the Cheng‑Prusoff approximation or per‑assay calibration constants where possible.
  • Discarded ambiguous units / ">" or "<" measurements.
  • Augmented with molecular dynamics trajectories generated by Recursion, providing motion examples for the diffusion to learn flexible binding modes.

Architecture: a "structure‑aware MLP" — sort of

The affinity head is structurally another stack of PairFormer layers, but the input is the filtered pair representation zij on protein–ligand and intra‑ligand pairs only. After several blocks, you pool the ligand atoms (e.g., via a learned attention readout), concatenate with a pooled protein‑pocket representation, and pass through two heads (sigmoid for binary, linear for regression). The whole thing is fine‑tuned on top of a pre‑trained Boltz‑2 trunk (the trunk is partially frozen during this stage).

Why this works at all
Two reasons. First, the structural pre‑training has already taught the network where binding pockets are and how a ligand's atoms tend to interact with surrounding side chains. Second, the affinity head doesn't have to predict from scratch — it gets a 3D pose plus a pair representation already enriched with chemistry. The hard work is mostly done.

9. Affinity prediction in context — Boltz‑2 vs FEP+

Why this section matters

If someone asks "is Boltz‑2 actually good at affinity?" you need a calibrated answer. The industry yardstick is FEP+ — a physics‑based method that's painfully slow but accurate. Knowing where Boltz‑2 wins (throughput) and loses (final‑round precision) is what keeps you from over‑claiming.

In plain English FEP+ is the careful chef who takes 12 hours per dish and gets it right. Boltz‑2 is the food‑truck cook who cranks out a million plates in the same time at 80% of the quality. Neither is "better"; they're for different stages of a campaign. Use Boltz‑2 to find the few hundred compounds worth cooking carefully — then run FEP+ on those.

The gold standard for computational binding affinity is Free Energy Perturbation (FEP). FEP simulates a thermodynamic path from one ligand to a closely related one using molecular dynamics, computes the free energy difference along the path, and is physically grounded — it includes all the entropy and explicit‑water effects that matter for ΔG. The flagship commercial implementation is Schrödinger's FEP+.

Reading this table the right way: Boltz‑2 will not replace FEP+ in the late stages of a campaign where you're optimizing the last 0.1 kcal/mol on a clinical candidate. It will replace FEP for everything before that — the screening, the scaffold hopping, the SAR triage where you have ~10⁵ compounds and need a ranking. That's the regime where 1000× speed at 80% of the accuracy is unambiguously a win.

What Boltz‑2 still gets wrong

  • Allosteric binders. If the ligand binds far from the active site, training data is sparse.
  • Charged species & metal coordination. Force‑field‑level effects that DDPMs don't model directly.
  • Very flexible ligands. Macrocycles & peptides — diffusion samples drift.
  • Out‑of‑distribution chemistry. Novel scaffolds the model never saw in training look like noise to it.
  • Cellular context. No model captures kinetics, off‑target effects, PK/ADME — that's still the chemist's job.
Common rookie mistake: reporting a single Boltz‑2 affinity value as if it were a measurement. It isn't. Always (1) sample multiple seeds and report the spread, (2) compare to the binary classifier — if the classifier says "probably not a binder," don't trust the regression number, and (3) anchor on a known binder for the same target so you have a sanity check. Treat Boltz‑2 like a triage tool, not a microscope.

10. Running Boltz‑2 in practice

Why this section matters

This is the only section that matters on day one. If you skipped everything above, start here: a pip install, a YAML file, and one command get you from sequence + SMILES to a 3D complex with affinity predictions. Bookmark this page.

The repo (jwohlwend/boltz) is pip‑installable:

# install
pip install boltz

# run with auto‑MSA via ColabFold's MMseqs2 server
boltz predict input.yaml --use_msa_server --output_format pdb

The input YAML for a protein + ligand affinity prediction looks like:

version: 1
sequences:
  - protein:
      id: A
      sequence: MTEYKLVVVGAGGVGKSALTIQLIQNHFVDEYDPTIEDSYRKQVVIDGETCLLDILDTAGQEEYSAMRDQYMRTGEGFLCVFAINNTKSFEDIHQYREQIKRVKDSDDVPMVLVGNKCDLAARTVESRQAQDLARSYGIPYIETSAKTRQGVEDAFYTLVREIRQH
      msa: empty          # or path to .a3m
  - ligand:
      id: L
      smiles: "CC(=O)Oc1ccccc1C(=O)O"   # aspirin

properties:
  - affinity:
      binder: L

That YAML alone (after the MSA is fetched) gets you, in ~20 s on a single A100:

  • complex.cif — predicted 3D structure of the K‑Ras / aspirin complex,
  • confidence.json — pLDDT, pAE per token,
  • affinity.json — {"affinity_pred_value": 1.83, "affinity_probability_binary": 0.21}

The affinity_pred_value: 1.83 means predicted log10(IC50/μM) ≈ 1.83, so IC50 ≈ 67 μM. The affinity_probability_binary: 0.21 says only ~21% likely to be a real binder — i.e., aspirin is not a K‑Ras binder, which is correct.

Practical tips

GPU memory
An A100 (40 GB) handles up to ~1500 tokens comfortably. For larger complexes use H100 or break into smaller chains.
Sample diversity
Default 5 diffusion samples; pick by pLDDT. For loops use 25.
Pocket constraints
If you know which residues line the binding site (from a homolog or from a docking experiment), pass them — accuracy goes up.
Affinity is harder than structure
Don't extrapolate. Boltz‑2 is calibrated on standard druglike chemistry; novel scaffolds, peptides, covalent binders need their own models.

How this fits into your summer

You'll probably use Boltz‑2 in two modes. Mode one: take a positively selected gene from the Ioerger lab's TB scans for which there's no PDB structure, fold it, look at the predicted pocket. Mode two: virtual‑screen a focused library of candidate inhibitors against an Mtb target and rank them by predicted IC50 before running any wet‑lab assays. Either way, the output is a starting point for an experimental campaign — not the final answer.
